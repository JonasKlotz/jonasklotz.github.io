Evaluating eXplainable AI methods for self-supervised representation learning

Finished project

This survey offers a comprehensive investigation of Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) techniques applied to self-supervised representation learning (SSRL). Several existing methods, namely VDSN, GradCAM for Embeddings, RELAX, Label-Free Feature Importance, and COCOA, are thoroughly evaluated using quantitative and qualitative analyses. Multiple pretrained models, including ResNet, VAE, SWaV, and SimCLR, are assessed on a synthetic dataset, and an additional evaluation is conducted by training SimCLR on this dataset. The findings highlight the strengths and limitations of these methods and discuss the associated challenges. Specifically, RELAX and COCOA demonstrate promising performance.

Noise Robust Multi-label Classifier for Satellite Images

Finished project

The rapid increase in Earth's satellite count has led to a surge in remote sensing data, making accurate labeling a significant challenge. We investigate the suitability of state-of-the-art computer vision methods for handling label noise in remote sensing data. Our research reveals that label message passing architectures outperform generic models, especially in dealing with subtractive and mixed label noise. The ASL loss function proves effective for high levels of label noise, and Weighted BCE outperforms BCE in handling subtractive and mixed noise. These findings provide insights into noise-robust methods for remote sensing and point to opportunities for refining techniques and applying them to other datasets. This research contributes to improving the accuracy of multi-label image classification in remote sensing and has implications for various applications in noisy environments.

Chess Vision: Extract Chess Positions from Images

Finished project

This project presents an algorithm designed for chessboard detection and piece recognition in images, with potential use in a mobile application to aid players in analyzing their games and enhancing their chess skills. The chessboard detection relies on a line-based approach, identifying the grid and calculating the corners based on clustered line intersections. For piece recognition, four convolutional neural network (CNN) architectures (Xception, ResnetV2, MobileNet, and NASNetMobile) were trained on a dataset containing 127,000 chess pieces. The board detection achieved an accuracy of approximately 41% within 0.85 seconds, compared to around 63% in 1.88 seconds when compared to a state-of-the-art algorithm. While the accuracy was about 22% lower, the execution time was reduced by a factor of approximately 2.2 times. The study examined different parameters, such as the number of output classes and the utilization of domain knowledge, to understand their impact on accuracy and F1 score for piece recognition. Among the tested configurations, the Xception network, with 13 output classes and domain knowledge, achieved the highest accuracy of around 57% for piece recognition.
